(cost expressed as % of wages)		 Legislation
Argentina Unemployment insurance premiums, expressed as a share of wages, go into the National Employment Fund. The cost varies across businesses as follows:

- Large businesses (Mediana Tramo 2) in the services sector (annual sales => ARS 19,012,110,000): 1.08%.

- Large businesses (Mediana Tramo 2) in the wholesale and retail sectors (annual sales => ARS 66,319,160,000): 1.08%.

- All other businesses: 0.94%.		 Law 20744 (1973).
Bolivia No mandatory unemployment protection.
Brazil 8% contribution over wage income deposited into an individual severance savings account. Law 8036 (1990) - Unemployment insurance.
Law 7988 (1990) - Severance.
Chile If the worker is under a fixed-term contract, 2.8% of the monthly wage is deposited into an individual severance account (this contribution is paid entirely by the employer). If the worker is under an open-term (indefinite) contract, 2.2% of the monthly wage goes to the individual severance account — 0.6% is paid by the employee and 1.6% by the employer. In addition, the employer pays a contribution to the Solidarity Unemployment Insurance Fund: 0.2% for fixed-term contracts and 0.8% for open-term contracts. Combined, total contributions equal 3.0% of wages. As of 2025, the contribution base is capped at 131.8 Unidades de Fomento (UF).

The Solidarity Fund is designed to help workers whose severance-account balances are insufficient to cover unemployment for a defined period — up to five months for workers on fixed-term contracts and up to three months for workers on open-term contracts.		 Law 20829 (2015).
Colombia Workers with earnings below 13MW: 1/12 monthly wage per month paid to individual severance savings account on January each year + 12% annual interest accrued on monthly dues, paid directly to worker. Law Decree 2663 (1950).
Dominican Republic No mandatory unemployment protection.
Ecuador 3% of wages to individual severance savings account. 2% paid by employee and 1% by employer. Independent workers who make voluntary contributions to SS also make this 3% contribution. Resolution No. CD 501 of the Board of Directors of the Institituto Ecuatoriano de Seguridad Social (2015).
Honduras Employers pay 4% over wages (maximum contribution base: 3 MW) to a severance savings account. Decree No. 56 (2015).
Mexico Modality: Authorization to use retirement savings during unemployment spans of more than 45 days.

Protection granted: Workers who entered the contributory pension system at least three years before the unemployment period and have contributed for at least 12 two-month periods since are entitled to receive the equivalent of 30 daily wages with a cap at 10 Federal MW.

Workers who entered the contributory pension system at least five years before the unemployment period are entitled to claim the minimum amount between 90 daily wages (averaged over the last 250 weeks) or 11.5% of their pension savings.		 Social Security Law (1995, last reformed in 2023).
Paraguay No mandatory unemployment protection.
Peru For workers in micro businesses: None.

For workers in small businesses: Half a monthly wage goes into a severance savings account.

For workers in medium and large businesses: One monthly wage goes to a severance savings account.		 Legislative Decree No. 871 (1996).

Sheet 4: TL ob
Country Other non-wage benefits Legislation
Argentina Mandatory life insurance: insurance premium of 0.205 per thousand of 5.5 MW. In June 2025, this premium was equal to ARS 334.68 per employee, or 0.001 MW. Decree 567 (1974).
Bolivia Profit sharing (yearly bonus): One annual monthly wage for employees in businesses with positive annual profits or a reduced amount to ensure the bonus cost doesn't exceed 25% of the firm's net profits.

Family allowances: Female employees will receive 4 family subsidies for insured mothers or beneficiaries: a prenatal subsidy equal to BOB 2,000 (0.73 MMW) for each of the last five months of pregnancy, paid in cash or in kind; a natality subsidy of BOB 2,000 as a one-time cash payment per child at birth; a lactation subsidy of BOB 2,000 provided in kind (mainly dairy or equivalent products) during the child’s first 12 months; and a funeral subsidy of BOB 2,000, a one-time cash payment upon the death of a dependent child under 19.		 Supreme Decree 224 (1943).
Brazil Transport subsidy: Employers must cover displacement costs for employees. They can deduct up to 6% of the employee's salary for this purpose. Calculations are done on a case-by-base basis depending on employee home location and displacement routes, and must be provided through in-kind vouchers. Law 7418 (2015).
Chile Profit Sharing: Employers must distribute 30% of the company's net taxable profits to employees annually. The distribution is proportional to each employee's remuneration. Alternatively, employers can pay a guaranteed annual bonus equivalent to 25% of each employee's annual salary. Profit sharing is capped at 4.75 MW. Law 18620 (1987).
Colombia Transport subsidy for workers earning up to 2MW: COP 200,000 in 2025.

Family subsidy: Monthly monetary transfer from Caja de Compensacion Familiar per dependent (18 or younger, 60+ or disabled) to workers earning 4 MW or less.		 Law 15 (1959) - Transport subsidy.
Law 21 (1982) - Family subsidy.
Dominican Republic Profit sharing: Businesses must distribute 10% of their annual profits among employees. Limits on individual profit shares depend on the length of service: workers in service for less than three years may only receive up to 45 days' worth of their regular salary, while workers in service for three years or more are entitled to 70 days' worth of their regular salary, at most. Employees contribute 0.5% of their profit shares to the National Institute providing technical and technological training to workers (INFOTEP). Law 16 (1992).
Ecuador Profit sharing: Businesses must distribute 15% of their annual profits among employees.

Individual Savings Accounts "Fondo de reserva": For employees with more than one year of service, employers pay one annual monthly wage into an individual savings account administered by the Social Security Administration.

Relocation expenses: Employers must pay for the relocation expenses of new hires and separated workers.		 Labor Code (2012).
Honduras No mandatory non-wage benefits.
Mexico Profit sharing: Businesses must distribute 10% of their annual profits among their employees. The first 5% is distributed evenly among all eligible employees based on the number of days worked. The second 5% is distributed to employees in proportion to their annual wages. As of 2025, the total employee's payment cannot exceed the highest between three salaries and the average payment of the last three years. Federal Labor Law (1970, last reformed in 2023).
Paraguay No mandatory non-wage benefits.
Peru Profit sharing: Businesses with more than 20 employees must share their annual pre-tax profits with their employees as follows:
- Businesses in mining, wholesale and retail businesses, and restaurants: 8%.
- Businesses in manufacturing, fishery and telecommunication: 10% .
- All other businesses: 5%.
The first half is distributed among employees based on the number of days worked. The second half is distributed to employees in proportion to their annual wages.

Life insurance: All employers must pay a life insurance premium equivalent to 0.53% of a monthly wage for white-collar employees, 0.71% for blue-collar employees, and 1.46% for those performing high-risk activities. These numbers are estimations published in the Legislative Decree, but the real values that risk insurers charge may vary. Workers' dependents receive 16 monthly wages in case of natural death, or 32 monthly wages in case of accidental death or permanent disability.		 Legislative Act No. 892 (1996) - Profit sharing.
Legislative Decree No. 688 (1991) - Life insurance.
United States No mandatory other non-wage benefits at the Federal or State level.
Spain No mandatory other non-wage benefits.
